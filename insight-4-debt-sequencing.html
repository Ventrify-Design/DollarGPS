Debt Sequencing: The Strategy Elite Banks Use — and Never Teach You

The order in which you pay off debt matters as much as how much you pay. Here's the mathematical framework that banks use internally — and keep off your statement.

Most people approach debt elimination the same way they approach a to-do list: tackle items in the order they appear, or pick whichever feels most urgent. Banks, of course, are intimately familiar with this tendency. It is one of the primary reasons consumer lending remains one of the most profitable products in financial services history.

What institutional lenders know — and what almost no retail borrower understands — is that debt has a sequencing dimension entirely separate from the balance or interest rate on any individual product. The optimal debt elimination strategy accounts for interest rate interactions, tax implications, credit score dynamics, and the opportunity cost of capital at every step. Executing it correctly can save tens of thousands of dollars compared to the most common consumer approaches.

The Two Camps: Avalanche vs. Snowball

Personal finance educators have long debated two approaches to debt repayment. The Avalanche method directs surplus cash to the highest interest rate debt first — mathematically optimal in terms of total interest paid. The Snowball method pays off the smallest balance first, regardless of interest rate — psychologically satisfying but mathematically suboptimal.

This debate, while useful, misses the more important question: what is the correct framework for deciding which debts to accelerate, maintain at minimum payments, or refinance — and in what order — given an individual's complete financial picture?

"A consumer paying off a 6% mortgage aggressively while carrying 22% credit card debt is doing the financial equivalent of patching the roof while the basement floods."

The Hidden Variable: Opportunity Cost

The core insight that separates sophisticated debt management from consumer-grade advice is opportunity cost sequencing. Every dollar you apply to debt repayment is a dollar not invested in an appreciating asset. Whether it is better to pay down a debt or invest the equivalent amount depends on the after-tax cost of the debt versus the after-tax expected return of the investment.

For most people, this calculation changes significantly based on debt type:

  • High-rate consumer debt (18–26% APR): Almost always worth eliminating aggressively before any discretionary investment. The guaranteed return of eliminating 22% debt exceeds any reasonable expected investment return.
  • Mid-rate personal loans (8–14% APR): Elimination competes with investment returns. Tax-advantaged investment contributions (pension, ISA) often win. Non-advantaged investments may not.
  • Low-rate mortgage debt (3–7% APR): In most market environments, investing in diversified equities outperforms aggressive mortgage repayment. The interest may also carry tax deductibility depending on jurisdiction.
  • Student debt with income-contingent repayment: In some jurisdictions, partial or full forgiveness after a set period changes the calculus entirely — overpaying may be financially irrational.

The Credit Score Interaction

A third variable that consumer debt advice frequently ignores is credit utilisation and its effect on borrowing cost. Credit scores affect not only whether you qualify for financing, but the interest rate at which you qualify — with differences of 2–3% on a mortgage representing $60,000–$90,000 in additional lifetime interest on a median property purchase.

This means that the optimal debt sequencing strategy sometimes involves maintaining certain balances or applying payments in a counterintuitive order to optimise the credit profile first, before pursuing pure mathematical optimisation of interest costs.

The Tax Dimension

Tax treatment of debt interest is frequently the most overlooked variable in debt sequencing. Mortgage interest may be deductible. Student loan interest has income-based deductibility in some jurisdictions. Business debt interest is often fully deductible. The after-tax cost of a 6% deductible debt for a higher-rate taxpayer may be effectively 3.6% — changing the opportunity cost calculation dramatically.

DollarGPS's engine accounts for each user's tax position when modelling debt sequencing. A strategy optimised for one tax bracket may be suboptimal for another, even with identical debt profiles.

Putting It Together: The Sequencing Framework

Effective debt sequencing requires answering five questions in order:

  1. What is the after-tax effective interest rate on each debt?
  2. Does any debt have income-contingent or forgiveness provisions that change the calculus?
  3. What is my current credit utilisation, and would rebalancing payments improve my score materially?
  4. What is my marginal expected return on incremental investment capital — particularly in tax-advantaged vehicles?
  5. Given all of the above, what is the optimal surplus allocation across debt repayment and investment each month?
